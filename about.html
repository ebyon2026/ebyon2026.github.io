4 TYPES OF ANTIDEPRESSANTS

Antidepressants are commonly classified into four main categories: tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs), monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs), selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs), and others. Each category targets different mechanisms of action in the brain, affecting the balance of neurotransmitters to alleviate symptoms of depression. TCAs and MAOIs are among the earliest types of antidepressants developed, while SSRIs and SNRIs are newer classes that generally have fewer side effects. The "others" category includes a variety of antidepressants that do not fit neatly into the aforementioned categories, each with unique properties and mechanisms of action.

SSRI (Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors):

Dosage: Inhibits the reuptake of serotonin, a neurotransmitter in the brain, leading to an increase in brain serotonin levels.

Features: Relatively low side effects and generally effective for most patients.

SNRI (Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors):

Dosage: Mechanism of Action: Inhibits the reuptake of both serotonin and norepinephrine, increasing the concentrations of these two neurotransmitters in the brain.

Features: Demonstrates high efficacy, but may occasionally cause side effects such as elevated blood pressure.

TCA (Tricyclic Antidepressants):

Mechanism of Action: Inhibits the reuptake of serotonin and norepinephrine while also affecting various neurotransmitter receptors.

Features: Associated with numerous side effects, often considered a last resort in the treatment of depression due to the availability of other options.

MAOI (Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors):

Mechanism of Action: Inhibits the action of the enzyme monoamine oxidase, preventing the breakdown of neurotransmitters like serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine.

Features: Usage may be challenging due to interactions with other drugs, dietary restrictions; typically reserved for cases unresponsive to other medications.
