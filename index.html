When adolescents take antidepressants, does the risk of suicide increase?

There have been reports that some individuals, especially adolescents, may experience increased suicidal thoughts and behaviors when taking antidepressants. The FDA has issued warnings regarding this. These side effects are particularly more pronounced in adolescents, who tend to show an increase in suicidal thoughts or actions.

Antidepressants have been in the headlines of U.S. news in recent years due to their association with adolescent suicide, leading to major pharmaceutical companies in the U.S. settling millions of dollars in lawsuits related to suicides. For instance, in 2009, pharmaceutical company GlaxoSmithKline (GSK) paid around $390 million to settle lawsuits related to suicides and suicide attempts associated with the antidepressant "Paxil." Similarly, manufacturers of popular antidepressants such as Lexapro and Celexa have settled 32 lawsuits related to suicide risks in the Eastern District Court of Missouri.

Why does the risk of suicide increase with antidepressant use?

Antidepressants are commonly used to alleviate symptoms of depression, but for some individuals, they can increase suicidal thoughts or behaviors as a side effect. This can occur due to various factors such as mood swings and worsening of depression.

Sudden mood swings to Aggravation of depression and anxiety

Depression is a complex issue influenced by biological, genetic, environmental, and psychological factors. SSRIs, which generally work by blocking the reuptake of serotonin in the brain, are thought to increase serotonin levels, a neurotransmitter believed to have positive effects on mood, sleep, and emotions.

However, this can lead to mood swings and worsen depression or anxiety. Particularly, there's a higher likelihood of experiencing suicidal thoughts and behaviors when taking more than the recommended dosage or abruptly discontinuing antidepressant use, or when combined with alcohol.

Restlessness, agitation, increased irritability

In some adolescents, taking antidepressants can induce anxiety, restlessness, irritability, agitation, or impulsive behavior. These side effects may indicate that the antidepressant is not effective, that depression is worsening, or as signs of suicidal impulses.

When taking SSRIs, some individuals (albeit a minority) may experience side effects such as violent behavior, mania, or aggression, all of which should be understood to potentially lead to suicide.

Increased anxiety when antidepressants are taken by individuals who don't necessarily need them

Some experts suggest that many individuals may not actually need antidepressants. If depression isn't severe, alternative therapies such as exercise or counseling... with minimal side effects, can be effective substitutes for antidepressants.

In 2014, a Danish research team published findings evaluating 13 clinical trials involving adults taking antidepressants. They found that patients who weren't clinically depressed before taking the medication could double their risk of suicide after taking it. The study revealed that administering antidepressants to such patients doubled the incidence of side effects like anxiety and nervousness, acting as "precursors to suicide or violence."

Are there antidepressants safe for adolescents?

Some antidepressants may be relatively safe for adolescents. However, the FDA recommends considering the risk of suicide for all antidepressants. While the FDA's clinical analysis only examined nine antidepressants, it issued the strongest safety warning for all antidepressants. Therefore, it's crucial to follow the doctor's instructions when prescribed antidepressants.

What adolescents should know when starting antidepressant medication:

The FDA recommends starting with the minimum effective dose of antidepressants and avoiding long-term use. Additionally, complementary therapies such as counseling or exercise should be considered.

Warning signs of self-harm/suicide:

During antidepressant use, adolescents may experience suicidal thoughts or behaviors as side effects. Therefore, parents or guardians should carefully monitor for warning signs of suicide or self-harm. If such signs appear, seeking immediate medical consultation is crucial.

Statistics related to suicide:

According to a 2022 report by the American Foundation for Suicide Prevention, over 44,000 people die by suicide each year in the United States, with about 15% of patients diagnosed with major depressive disorder and receiving medication therapy ultimately dying by suicide.

Conclusion

If prescribed antidepressants, It's important not just to pick up the medication but also to discuss and thoroughly understand the treatment goals, duration, expected outcomes of treatment, and any other beneficial therapies or complementary approaches with the doctor.

Only take the necessary minimum dose and avoid long-term use. The FDA recommends prescribing the minimum possible dose (to reduce the risk of intentional or accidental overdose in adolescents).

Moreover, at home, it's essential to carefully inspect and manage medications to prevent adolescents from hoarding and potentially taking them all at once. Additionally, close monitoring and discussions with parents, siblings, and primary caregivers are crucial, especially during the first few months of starting medication.

When adolescents are most at risk of suicide:

In the first few months after starting antidepressant treatment

When increasing or decreasing the dosage

When taking medication indiscriminately

Parents/Users should closely observe their children/user themselves every day during these critical periods and have conversations to detect any changes. It's not uncommon for parents to sleep alongside their children at night during the initial months of starting medication if their child has had suicidal or self-harming thoughts.
